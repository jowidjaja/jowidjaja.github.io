Today, I had two kayaks strapped to the top of my car, remnants of the weekend trek to Stallings island to go find and feed the donkeys. As I turned onto an interstate road, I was faced with an unforeseen challenger, a 55mph speed limit. 

I figured the straps would hold and pressed my foot into the accelerator, cautiously watching the tip of the kayaks from the windscreen. They remained in place, but a loud groan began to perpetuate outside my window.

These signals triggered a commencement of mini experiments. First, I began with acceleration and deceleration. As expected, the groans matched the velocity of my car. Maybe the straps were just loose? I tugged on them overhead, but instead of resolving the groans, I heard an increase in pitch. Curious. Reaching outside the window, I grasped the vibrating strap. Noteably, the groans muted as energy shifted to my hand. 

I finally decided it was probably a good idea for me to stop. Pulling into a gas station, I whipped out my phone and asked Google’s Gemini about the phenomenon of groaning kayak straps. It quickly responded with a term called “vortex shedding” and recommended twisting the parts of the straps not directly touching the kayaks. With a quick confirmation from the trusty source of Reddit, I twisted in a few turns and proceeded onto the road. To my delight, the suggestion worked and I was met with the absence of the groaning vibrations.

Later that day, I began my spiral down vortex shedding, a phenomenon *(probably well understood by aerospace nerds)* where wind passing by flat straps induce vibrations. As air is separated by the straps, whirlpools of low pressure form on each side of the strap in an alternating fashion leading to this high-speed vibration resulting in acoustic resonance. According to Gemini, “every taut object has a natural frequency at which it wants to vibrate when disturbed *(like a tuned guitar string)* and when these frequencies align *(frequency of shedding vortices and frequency of tight strap)*, resonance occurs.” 

What's interesting to me was not necessarily the physics itself, but the idea that resonance requires structure. It reminded me of a recent encounter I had with someone whose mind operated much like my own. That, when two people of the same frequency connect, it has the capacity to build resonance. However, much like the strap, if done at such uncontrolled intensity for prolonged periods of time, it may result in irrecoverable damage. Thus, resonance, something that can produce sound in a strap and love between two individuals, must be bounded and contained to preserve the integrity of both interacting structures. On another note,   can refuse to embody the fullness of who you are meant to be as an independent entity. An identity remains unexplored. The strap remains slack. It is indeed safe. But it comes at the cost of never truly producing resonance.
